Sustainable Agriculture: Methods, Impacts, Involvement

Soil Health

The soil is a very important but often disregarded carbon sink. Worldwide, the soil sequesters around 1500 billion tonnes of carbon dioxide (europa.eu). Poor agricultural practices that degrade the soil essentially turn it into dirt without the ability to sequester carbon. These practices include:

  • Monocropping: Planting only one type of crop year after year on the same soil; this strips the same nutrients from the soil
  • Over tilling and plowing: This compresses the top layer of soil from heavy machinery and breaks apart soil, making it more susceptible to erosion
  • Flood Irrigation: the process of flooding an entire field to water crops is flood irrigation. This causes waterlogging of the soil and can also strip away the top layer of soil due to erosion
  • Leaving soil bare between season: This leaves soil open to the elements (wind, rain) so it is eroded

Good Practices Include:

  • Crop rotation: Planting different crops for each season ensures that the nutrients of the soil are never fully depleted and gives a chance for them to regenerate
  • No-till agriculture: this alleviates the compaction and erosion of the soil
  • Drip irrigation: this reduces soil erosion due to runoff (while also saving water!)
  • Cover crops: Covering the fields between seasons protects the soil by holding it in place with roots and shielding it from rain

Water Cleanliness

Eutrophication is the excessive accumulation of nutrients in a water way (Panayides). Common nutrients used for agricultural fertilizers are combinations of nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium. A 2025 study tested the effect of a common fertilizer, ammonium nitrate, on dissolved oxygen levels in a freshwater ecosystem. The study found that ammonium nitrate concentrations of 15 mg/L lowered dissolved oxygen significantly in a 500 mL environment over just a 4 week process (Panayides). Reduced dissolved oxygen levels can render an aquatic habitat inhabitable for many species.



Traditional, largescale agriculture not only harms aquatic habitats, but can also harm human health. A 2025 research study analyzed the extent to which anthropogenic arsenic pollution impacted lung cancer rates. The study found that agricultural runoff and drainage of waterwells (primarily for irrigaiton) contributed to excess arsenic concentrations in water (Panayides). Arsenic ingested from water sources was found to increase rates of lung cancer (Panayides).

Solutions

  • Using organic fertilizers (like compost) as apposed to synthetic fertilizers can reduce the efects of eutrophication
  • Using drip irrigation systems reduces runoff, as well as water waste draining from natural resovoirs

Biodiversity

The UN Environment Program sites the fertilizer and pesticide use in agriculture as the culprit for major biodiversity loss. In addition, they also site land clearing for farm land as another major degrader of biodiversity (UNEP).

Solutions

  • Using organic practices eliminates the use of fertilizers
  • Farming organically also reduces the spread of GMO crops, preserving natural biodiveristy
  • Preserving soil health eliminates the need to continually clear more land for agriculture
